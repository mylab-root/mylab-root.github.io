Identify transcripts for adaptation to abiotic stress in developmental zones

The balance of the ROS signals, superoxide in the meristematic zone and hydrogen peroxide in the differentiation zone, determines the transition from cell division to cell elongation (Fig. 1). However, this balance is altered during periods of stress. For example, with short exposure (3 hours) to salt stress conditions, there is more hydrogen peroxide produced in the meristematic zone, which inhibits root growth and promotes premature differentiation. This suggests that hydrogen peroxide signals mediate root phenotypes under salt stress. Other abiotic stresses produce hydrogen peroxide in specific developmental zones or cell types.

My research goal is to elucidate how plants coordinate spatial distribution of ROS signals in the root when undergoing abiotic stress and how this helps to adapt to the stress. To understand the coordination mechanism of gene expression among the developmental zones under stress, I will generate spatiotemporal transcriptome analyses. It has been shown that expression of alternatively spliced isoforms and long intergenic non-coding (linc) RNA are very responsive to abiotic stress. However, the function of the majority of these RNAs remains unknown. I will identify all splice isoforms of protein coding genes and lincRNAs that are responsive to abiotic stress in the three developmental zones.
